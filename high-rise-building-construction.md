# How High-Rise Buildings Are Built in Australia: A Site Engineer's Guide

**By Jay Sah** | Site Engineer | Sydney, NSW  
*Published: April 2026*

---

I have spent 4+ years on high-rise construction sites in Sydney — from large-scale infrastructure with a Tier 1 contractor to delivering a $300M+ residential tower project with a Tier 2 developer. This article is a practical breakdown of how high-rise buildings are actually constructed in Australia, written from a site engineer's perspective.

If you are a graduate engineer, a student of structural engineering, or someone curious about what happens behind the hoarding, this guide walks you through every stage — from the ground up.

---

## What Qualifies as a High-Rise?

In Australia, the National Construction Code (NCC) classifies a building as high-rise when its **effective height exceeds 25 metres** — roughly 8 storeys or more. These buildings fall under **Type A construction** and are subject to the most stringent fire safety, structural, and egress requirements in the code.

Most high-rise residential projects in Sydney are **Class 2 buildings** (apartments) under NCC Volume One. The standards governing their design and construction include:

- **AS/NZS 1170** — Structural actions (wind, earthquake, dead and live loads)
- **AS 3600** — Concrete structures
- **AS 4100** — Steel structures
- **AS 1530** — Fire testing methods
- **AS 2118** — Sprinkler systems
- **AS 4072.1** — Fire-resistant service penetrations

These standards, together with the NCC Performance Requirements, form the compliance framework every site engineer works within.

## Stage 1: Planning, Design, and Approvals

Before a single excavator touches the site, years of planning take place.

### Development Application (DA) and Construction Certificate (CC)

The developer submits a DA to the local council or a planning panel. For major high-rise projects in Sydney, this often goes through the **Independent Planning Commission** or the local council's gateway process.

Once approved, a **Construction Certificate (CC)** is issued by a certifier (private or council), confirming the design complies with the NCC and relevant Australian Standards.

### Structural Design

The structural engineer designs the building's load path — how gravity and lateral forces travel from the roof down to the foundations.

For high-rise residential buildings in Sydney, the most common structural system is a **reinforced concrete core and flat plate slab** arrangement:

- **Core walls** (lift shafts, stair shafts) resist lateral loads from wind and earthquake
- **Flat plate slabs** span between the core and perimeter columns
- **Transfer structures** (beams or slabs) redistribute loads where column layouts change between podium and tower levels

The design follows **AS 3600:2018** for concrete and **AS/NZS 1170** for load combinations. Key design checks include:

- Axial shortening of columns and core walls
- Punching shear at slab-column connections
- Inter-storey drift under wind and seismic loads
- Robustness and progressive collapse resistance

### Geotechnical Investigation

Before foundation design, a geotechnical engineer investigates the site. In Sydney, ground conditions vary significantly — from sandstone bedrock in the CBD and Eastern Suburbs to shale and clay in Western Sydney and the Hills District.

Borehole logs, CPT testing, and lab analysis determine:

- Allowable bearing pressures
- Groundwater levels
- Rock quality designation (RQD)
- Excavation methodology

## Stage 2: Demolition and Site Establishment

### Demolition

If existing structures are on site, demolition is carried out under a **Demolition Work Plan** and in compliance with **AS 2601 (Demolition of Structures)**. This includes asbestos surveys, removal of hazardous materials, and progressive dismantling.

### Site Establishment

Once the site is cleared, the following are set up:

- **Hoarding and fencing** to secure the perimeter
- **Site offices, amenities, and storage** for the project team
- **Crane pads and tower crane bases** — the crane base is one of the first structural elements designed and built
- **Traffic management plans** for construction vehicle access
- **Environmental controls** — sediment fences, dust suppression, and noise monitoring

A **Construction Environmental Management Plan (CEMP)** and **Work Health and Safety Management Plan (WHSMP)** are required before any work begins.

---

## Stage 3: Excavation and Shoring

High-rise buildings in Sydney typically have 2–4 levels of basement for car parking, services, and storage.

### Bulk Excavation

Excavation is staged and supervised by the geotechnical engineer. In rock, this involves:

- **Rock hammering** or **controlled blasting** (rare in urban Sydney)
- **Rock sawing** for clean faces adjacent to boundaries

In soil, **shoring systems** are installed to support the excavation walls:

- **Contiguous or secant piled walls** — overlapping bored piles forming a retaining wall
- **Soldier piles and lagging** — H-beams with timber or concrete lagging
- **Sheet piling** — interlocking steel sheets driven into the ground
- **Anchored walls** — ground anchors or soil nails providing lateral support

### Dewatering

If groundwater is encountered above the basement level, dewatering is required. This typically involves **wellpoints or sump pumps** with water treatment and discharge in accordance with **EPA and council requirements**.

## Stage 4: Foundations

The foundation type depends on the building loads and ground conditions.

### Common Foundation Types for High-Rise

| Foundation Type | When Used | Key Standard |
|---|---|---|
| **Bored piles** | Deep foundations to rock or competent bearing layer | AS 2159 |
| **Pad footings** | Where rock is shallow and bearing capacity is high | AS 3600 |
| **Raft slab** | Where loads are distributed and settlement must be controlled | AS 3600 |
| **Pile caps** | To group piles under heavily loaded columns or core walls | AS 3600, AS 2159 |

On the **Skyview Project** in Castle Hill, the ground conditions in the Hills District required careful foundation design to accommodate variable shale depths and ensure differential settlement was within acceptable limits.

### Pile Testing

Piles are tested to verify load capacity:

- **Static load testing** — applying incremental loads to a test pile
- **Dynamic testing (PDA)** — using a drop hammer to measure pile response
- **Integrity testing** — low-strain impact to check for defects

## Stage 5: Substructure (Basement Construction)

Once foundations are in place, the basement structure is built from the lowest level upward.

### Construction Sequence

1. **Basement slab (raft or slab-on-ground)** — poured on prepared subgrade with waterproofing membrane below
2. **Basement columns and walls** — formed, reinforced, and poured
3. **Suspended basement slabs** — formwork, reinforcement, and concrete pour for each level
4. **Ramps and access ways** — connecting basement levels

### Waterproofing

Basement waterproofing is critical and one of the most common sources of defects in high-rise construction. Typical systems include:

- **Below-slab membrane** (sheet or spray-applied) — applied before the slab pour
- **Tanking systems** — external waterproofing on retaining walls
- **Water stops** at construction joints — PVC or hydrophilic strips
- **Drainage cells and geo-fabric** behind retaining walls

The NCC 2025 is consolidating its water management provisions (Parts F1 and F3) to improve clarity around waterproofing performance requirements — a welcome change for site engineers who deal with water ingress issues daily.

## Stage 6: Superstructure (Structure Above Ground)

This is the most visible and repetitive phase of high-rise construction. The structure rises floor by floor.

### Typical Floor Cycle

On a well-run high-rise project, the **floor cycle** — the time to complete one typical floor — is usually **5 to 7 working days**. This includes:

1. **Core walls** — Jump form or slip form advances the core ahead of the slabs (usually 2–3 floors ahead)
2. **Columns** — Formed, reinforced, and poured on each level
3. **Slab formwork** — Table forms (flying forms) or conventional formwork placed for the floor slab
4. **Slab reinforcement** — Bottom bars, top bars, and shear reinforcement placed and tied. Post-tensioning tendons installed if the slab is PT
5. **Services rough-in** — Electrical conduits and plumbing sleeves cast into the slab
6. **Concrete pour** — Slab poured using a concrete pump (boom or line pump)
7. **Curing** — Slab cured for minimum 7 days (as per AS 3600)
8. **Formwork strip** — Forms stripped and repositioned for the next floor

### Core Construction

The **core** is the backbone of a high-rise building. It contains:

- Lift shafts
- Fire-isolated stairwells
- Services risers
- Structural shear walls for lateral load resistance

Core walls are typically constructed using **jump formwork** (hydraulic climbing formwork) that lifts itself from floor to floor. The core advances ahead of the floor slabs to allow crane access and structural stability during construction.

### Concrete Supply and Pumping

High-rise concrete is typically **high-strength (50–65 MPa for columns and core walls, 32–40 MPa for slabs)** and pumped to height using:

- **Boom pumps** at lower levels
- **Static line pumps** with fixed pipework for upper levels
- **Placing booms** mounted on the building structure at height

Concrete is delivered by agitator trucks and must be placed within the allowable time limits specified by the concrete supplier and AS 3600.

## Stage 7: Facade and External Envelope

Once the structure has advanced sufficiently (typically 5–10 floors ahead of the facade), external cladding installation begins.

### Common Facade Systems in Sydney High-Rise

- **Precast concrete panels** — factory-made, crane-lifted into position, and fixed to slab edges
- **Curtain wall systems** — aluminium-framed glass panels spanning floor-to-floor
- **Rendered and painted masonry** — blockwork infill walls with acrylic render
- **Composite panel systems** — aluminium composite panels (ACPs) on a support frame (now heavily regulated after the Grenfell and Lacrosse cladding fires)

### Facade Compliance

Post-Grenfell, Australian regulations have tightened significantly on external wall cladding. The NCC requires:

- **Non-combustible materials** for external walls of buildings over 2 storeys (Type A and B construction)
- Compliance with **AS 5113 (Fire propagation testing)** for facade systems
- Evidence of suitability through testing, certification, or expert assessment

The **Building Commission NSW** has been active since December 2023 in enforcing compliance and elevating standards for facade materials and installation.

### Fire Stopping at Facade

At the slab edge where the facade meets the structure, **perimeter fire stopping** is installed to prevent fire spread between floors. This is a critical detail that must be inspected on every floor.

---

## Stage 8: Internal Fit-Out and Services

Once a floor is structurally complete and the facade is installed above it, internal fit-out begins. This phase involves multiple trades working concurrently.

### Fit-Out Sequence (Typical)

| Stage | Trades Involved |
|---|---|
| **1. First fix** | Framing (steel stud walls), electrical conduit, plumbing rough-in, mechanical ductwork, fire services piping |
| **2. Lining** | Plasterboard installation and finishing |
| **3. Wet areas** | Waterproofing (AS 3740), tiling, shower screens |
| **4. Second fix** | Joinery (kitchens, wardrobes), electrical fittings, plumbing fixtures, painting |
| **5. Floor coverings** | Carpet, timber, vinyl, tiles |
| **6. Final fix** | Hardware, mirrors, touch-up, cleaning |

### Wet Area Waterproofing

Waterproofing of bathrooms, laundries, and balconies must comply with **AS 3740**. This is one of the most inspection-heavy activities on a high-rise project. Common hold points include:

- Substrate preparation and falls check
- Membrane application (minimum 2 coats)
- Flash testing or flood testing before tiling
- Junction details at walls, hobs, and penetrations

### Fire Services

Fire services installation includes:

- **Sprinkler systems** (AS 2118)
- **Hydrant systems** (AS 2419)
- **Smoke detection and alarm systems** (AS 1670)
- **Fire dampers and smoke dampers** in ductwork
- **Emergency lighting and exit signage** (AS 2293)

Each system is commissioned and tested before the building can receive an **Occupation Certificate (OC)**.

---

## Stage 9: External Works and Landscaping

While internal fit-out progresses, external works are completed:

- **Driveways and access roads**
- **Stormwater drainage** and connection to council infrastructure
- **Landscaping, retaining walls, and fencing**
- **Swimming pools, BBQ areas, and communal facilities** (common in large residential projects like Skyview)
- **Footpath and crossover reinstatement**

---

## Stage 10: Commissioning, Inspection, and Handover

### Commissioning

All building services are commissioned — tested and verified to operate as designed:

- Lifts
- Fire systems (sprinklers, hydrants, smoke detection, emergency warning)
- Mechanical ventilation and air conditioning
- Electrical switchboards, metering, and emergency power
- Hydraulic systems (hot water, cold water, gas, sewer)

### Final Inspections

The **Principal Certifying Authority (PCA)** conducts final inspections and reviews:

- Compliance with the approved Construction Certificate drawings
- Fire safety schedule completeness
- Essential services commissioning reports
- Structural adequacy certificates

### Occupation Certificate (OC)

Once all inspections are passed and compliance documentation is complete, the PCA issues an **Occupation Certificate** — allowing residents to move in.

For staged developments like Skyview (multiple towers), OCs may be issued per building or per stage.

## NCC 2025: What Is Changing for High-Rise Construction?

The NCC 2025 was published on **1 February 2026**, with possible adoption from **1 May 2026** depending on each state and territory.

Key changes relevant to high-rise construction include:

- **Increased energy efficiency standards** — stricter HVAC requirements, mandatory solar PV on certain building classes, and EV charging infrastructure provisions
- **Improved condensation management** — stricter external wall requirements in various climate zones and updated roof ventilation provisions
- **Strengthened fire safety** — removal of Expert Judgement for fire safety, mandatory sprinklers in carparks with 40+ cars, and tighter cladding regulations
- **Better water management** — consolidation of waterproofing and cladding provisions into a single streamlined section
- **Healthier living environments** — stricter indoor air quality standards and improved lighting and ventilation requirements

Residential-only changes beyond this edition have been paused until mid-2029, giving the industry time to adapt.

## The Role of a Site Engineer on a High-Rise Project

As a site engineer, my daily responsibilities span across all of these stages:

- **Reviewing and interpreting structural and architectural drawings**
- **Conducting QA/QC inspections** — 100+ per week on a project like Skyview, logged in Procore
- **Managing ITPs (Inspection and Test Plans)** — ensuring hold points are released before work proceeds
- **Coordinating subcontractors** — 8+ trades working simultaneously on structure, facade, and fit-out
- **Ensuring NCC and Australian Standards compliance** — from concrete cover to fire stopping
- **Managing defects** — identifying, documenting, and tracking rectification
- **Liaising with the certifier, consultants, and the project manager** on technical issues

A good site engineer does not just inspect — they anticipate problems, understand the design intent, and keep the project moving safely and compliantly.

---

## Final Thoughts

Building a high-rise is one of the most complex undertakings in construction. Every floor is a coordination exercise involving structural engineering, fire safety, services integration, and quality management — all happening under time and cost pressure.

Understanding the full construction sequence — and where the risks sit — is what separates an effective site engineer from someone who is simply present on site.

If you are starting your career in high-rise construction in Australia, invest time in learning the NCC, the key Australian Standards (AS 3600, AS 1170, AS 1530), and how to use tools like Procore and Bluebeam. The technical knowledge compounds over time, and the industry needs engineers who understand both the design and the delivery.
